# Mailbox warmup explained

Warmup is the reason cold email tools tell you to wait before sending. This article explains what it is, why it exists, and what you can actually send on day one with Mirvo — without the wait, when you start from your own mailbox.

## Why warmup exists

Mailbox providers like Gmail and Outlook judge a sender by behavior. A fresh domain or mailbox that suddenly sends hundreds of emails a day looks like a spammer, so it gets filtered. To avoid that, a new domain has to build reputation gradually: it sends a low volume at first, then increases over several weeks as providers learn to trust it.

This is real and unavoidable for a **new** domain. There's no trick that skips it — anyone who promises a brand-new domain at full volume on day one is setting you up for the spam folder.

## How Mirvo lets you send on day one anyway

The shortcut isn't skipping warmup — it's **not starting from a cold domain in the first place.**

When you connect your own Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 mailbox, that mailbox is already in daily use and already has reputation. There's nothing to warm up, so you can start sending the same day. This is the recommended way to begin, and it's why your first campaign can go out right after setup.

The trade-off is volume: to protect your main address, Mirvo caps daily sending on a personal mailbox to a low number. Great for testing and steady low-volume outreach, not for high volume.

<Callout type="tip">
Start small. For your first batch, send to 20–50 verified prospects so your account establishes a clean, consistent pattern before you scale up.
</Callout>

## When warmup does apply: a dedicated domain

When you move to a dedicated sending domain (Mirvo registers and manages it for you), that domain is brand new — so warmup applies to it. **Plan on around four weeks** before it runs at full volume. Sending starts low and ramps up gradually as the domain earns reputation.

You're not stuck waiting during those weeks. Your connected personal mailbox keeps carrying your real outreach as a bridge while the dedicated domain warms up in the background. The dedicated domain takes on volume gradually, and by around week four it's running at full capacity.

<Callout type="info">
Warmup is a gradual ramp, never a switch. A new domain takes on real volume a little at a time as it earns trust — pushing it to full volume overnight is exactly what triggers spam filtering.
</Callout>

## What "warmup progress" means in your dashboard

When a dedicated domain is warming up, your dashboard shows its progress:

- **Warming (0–30%)** — early reputation building, lowest volume.
- **Building (30–70%)** — reputation growing, volume ramping up.
- **Ready (70–100%)** — trusted, running at or near full volume.
