# Your Master ICP

The Master ICP is the single most important setting in Mirvo. Every email Mirvo drafts uses it as the ground truth for tone, relevance, and the pain angle. A vague ICP produces generic drafts. A sharp ICP produces emails that get replies.

This article covers how to fill it in correctly, with concrete good/bad examples.

## The three sections

### 1. Who you sell to

Job titles, company size, industry, and any other qualifier that defines your target. The more specific, the better.

**Too vague:**
> "B2B companies with a sales team"

**Better:**
> "VP of Sales or Head of Revenue at SaaS companies, 20-200 employees, Series A to B, selling to SMBs. They're running a 3-5 person sales team without a dedicated SDR. Usually using HubSpot or Salesforce."

The second version tells Mirvo what the prospect's situation actually looks like. That context flows directly into personalization.

**Common mistake:** copying your marketing site's "ideal customer" section. Marketing copy is written for scanning, not for AI context. Write it like you're briefing a new salesperson on day one.

### 2. What pain you solve

Not a feature list. A specific outcome the prospect cares about, written in the language they'd use themselves.

**Too vague:**
> "We help companies improve sales efficiency"

**Better:**
> "The VP of Sales is spending 3 hours a week on list-building that their reps should be doing, or they've tried an SDR who ramped slowly and left. They need pipeline without the overhead. Mirvo gets them 5-10 qualified conversations per month without headcount."

Include the language your best prospects use when describing their problem. If you've talked to customers, quote them.

### 3. Tone of voice

Three to five examples of how you'd write to a prospect. This isn't about formal vs casual. It's about the specific phrases and attitudes that match your brand and your buyers.

**Example (good):**
> - "We've done this ourselves, so I know what doesn't work."
> - "No fluff, here's what we do and why it might matter to you."
> - "Straight line: you should try this if X, skip it if Y."

Avoid generic descriptors like "professional but friendly." Give Mirvo actual sentences to model.

## How ICP impacts drafts

When Mirvo drafts an email, it combines:

1. The prospect's data (company, role, recent activity, signal context if active)
2. Your Master ICP (who you sell to, their pain, your tone)

The ICP shapes the angle, the pain framing, and the call to action. A prospect who matches your ICP precisely gets a highly specific email. A prospect who's only a partial match gets a more general draft. That's by design: Mirvo doesn't invent relevance.

<Callout type="tip">
After your first batch of drafts, read 5 of them critically. If they sound generic, the ICP is the first thing to sharpen.
</Callout>

## What not to put in the ICP

- Feature lists (save for your sequence copy)
- Company history or founding story
- Competitive comparisons
- Aspirational language ("we're on a mission to...")

The ICP should describe the buyer, not your company.

## Updating the ICP

You can update it any time. Updates apply to new drafts generated after the change. Existing approved drafts are not affected.

Best practice: update the ICP after every 10 conversations where you learn something new about your buyers' language or pain points.
