# Your ICP

Your ICP is the single most important setting in Mirvo. Every email Mirvo drafts uses it as ground truth for tone, relevance, and pain framing. A vague ICP produces generic drafts. A sharp ICP produces emails that get replies.

You edit it in the **Profile & ICP** tab (second entry in the sidebar), in the section labelled **Ideal Customer Profile**. Direct link: [dashboard/profile#icp](/dashboard/profile#icp).

The fastest way to bootstrap it is right above, in the **Your Offer** section: paste your website URL, click **Auto-fill**. Mirvo parses your site and pre-fills both the Offer and the ICP fields in a single click. Review, sharpen, save. Nothing beats a two-minute review after auto-fill.

## How to sharpen each field

The ICP block is one free-text description at the top, followed by structured criteria. Fill both; the free-text description is what the AI uses to reason about relevance, and the structured criteria are what filtering, targeting, and Signals hook onto.

### ICP description

A plain-English description of your buyer: who they are, the situation they're in, and what they care about. Concrete, in their language, not marketing copy.

**Too vague:**
> "B2B companies with a sales team"

**Better:**
> "VP of Sales or Head of Revenue at SaaS companies, 20-200 employees, Series A to B, selling to SMBs. They run a 3-5 person sales team with no dedicated SDR. Usually using HubSpot or Salesforce."

The second version tells Mirvo what the prospect's situation actually looks like. That context flows directly into personalization.

**Common mistake:** copying your marketing site's "ideal customer" section. Marketing copy is written for scanning, not for AI context. Write it like you're briefing a new salesperson on day one.

Once the description reads right, click **Regenerate criteria**. Mirvo reads the description and populates the structured fields below (target titles, industries, regions, company sizes, pain points, tone). Review each pill and text field, correct anything that drifts.

### Target titles, industries, regions, company sizes, revenue

These are what Mirvo uses to filter prospects and match Signals. Keep them tight. Ten specific titles beats a shortlist that includes "any sales or growth role." A single industry beats "SaaS or Fintech or Marketplaces." Overly broad criteria drag reply rates down because you send to prospects who don't fit.

**Company sizes** and **target revenue** are multi-select pills. Pick the ranges you actually sell into today.

### Pain points

Not a feature list. A specific outcome the prospect cares about, written in the language they'd use themselves.

**Too vague:**
> "We help companies improve sales efficiency"

**Better:**
> "The VP of Sales is spending 3 hours a week on list-building that their reps should be doing, or they've tried an SDR who ramped slowly and left. They need pipeline without the overhead. Mirvo gets them 5-10 qualified conversations per month without headcount."

Include the language your best prospects use when describing the problem. Quote them if you have call notes.

### Tone

The tone selector has five presets: **Professional**, **Casual**, **Direct**, **Friendly**, **Witty**. Pick the one that matches how you'd actually talk to a prospect on a first call. If you want to shape voice more finely, add the campaign-level tone override at launch time.

## How ICP impacts drafts

When Mirvo drafts an email, it combines:

1. The prospect's data (company, role, recent activity, Signal context if active)
2. Your ICP (description, structured criteria, pain points, tone)

The ICP shapes the angle, the pain framing, and the call to action. A prospect who matches your ICP precisely gets a highly specific email. A prospect who's only a partial match gets a more general draft. That's by design: Mirvo doesn't invent relevance.

<Callout type="tip">
After your first batch of drafts, read five of them critically. If they sound generic, the ICP is the first thing to sharpen. Update the description, click Regenerate criteria, save.
</Callout>

## What not to put in the ICP

- Feature lists (save for your sequence copy)
- Company history or founding story
- Competitive comparisons
- Aspirational language ("we're on a mission to...")

The ICP describes the buyer, not your company.

## Updating the ICP

Open [dashboard/profile#icp](/dashboard/profile#icp). Edit the description or the structured fields directly, then click **Save ICP**. Updates apply to new drafts generated after the change. Existing approved drafts are not affected.

Best practice: update the ICP after every ten conversations where you learn something new about your buyers' language or pain points.
